Biography

Simone de Beauvoir was one of the most influential and progressive French writers/philosophers of her time. She was most famous for her writings that tackled social standards, feminism, politics, and ethics.


Early Life

Born in Paris, France, to Georges Bertrand de Beauvoir and Françoise (née) Brasseur, Simone was a very curious child with a thirst for knowledge. She would teach her younger sister, Hélène, as a student. Her father would also introduce her to various forms of literature, and encouraged her to learn and read as much as possible. However, Simone did not agree with her mother's love for her bourgeois lifestyle. Simone was enrolled in the Institut Adeline Désir to further her Catholic education. During this time, she befriended a girl named Elizabeth Mabille (Zaza). The two were extremely close until Zaza's untimely death in 1929, which Simone believed to be because of the stress of an arranged marriage. This tradition was very popular in the bourgeois lifestyle, and the incident only feuled Simone's strong beliefs against the culture.


Career

Simone de Beauvoir did very well in school. She attended the Sorbonne and passed her agrégation in philosophy in 1929. It was here that she also met her lifelong partner and friend, Jean-Paul Sartre. Simone became a teacher for over a decade before founding and editing Le Temps modernes, a monthly review.


Writing

Simone de Beauvoir's writing tackled many progressive subjects of her time. Her works were focused around Existentialism, and she saught to analyze the structure of society and politics. She challenged the belief of the stereotypical woman and her role in the world, and brought to light the truths of women's oppression. She also explored how theater, literature, and media contribute to the reformation of society.
